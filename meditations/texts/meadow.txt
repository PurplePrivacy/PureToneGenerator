You are walking through tall grass toward the center of a vast summer meadow.
The grass reaches your waist and brushes softly against your hands as you move through it.
Each step releases the warm, green scent of crushed stems and sun-warmed earth.
The sky above is a deep, unbroken blue, the blue of midsummer at its fullest.
A few white clouds drift so slowly they seem suspended, barely moving at all.

You find a soft, level spot and lie down in the grass.
The tall stems close around you like gentle curtains, creating a private green room.
Above you, the grass tips sway against the sky, tracing slow circles in the breeze.
The ground beneath you is warm and firm, heated by a full day of summer sun.
You feel its warmth radiating upward through your back and into your body.

Your shoulders settle into the earth, and you feel the ground accept your weight.
Your spine softens against the warm soil, each vertebra finding its own resting place.
Your arms lie at your sides, palms upward, fingers gently curled.
The warm earth beneath your hands feels alive, and you can almost sense its slow pulse.
You are held completely by the ground, and there is nothing you need to do.

Wildflowers surround you in every direction, scattered through the grass like scattered jewels.
You see purple clover, yellow buttercups, white daisies with their bright yellow centers.
Orange poppies nod on slender stems, their petals as thin and delicate as tissue.
Blue cornflowers stand tall among the grasses, their color as vivid as the sky above.
Each flower is perfect in its own way, and none of them is trying to be anything else.

A bee lands on a clover bloom near your head, its body heavy with golden pollen.
You can hear the low, contented buzz of its wings as it works the tiny florets.
The sound is steady and warm, the sound of summer itself made audible.
Other bees move through the meadow, each one visiting flower after flower.
Their combined hum creates a soft, ambient drone that blends with the warmth.

A butterfly drifts past on painted wings, following an invisible path through the air.
It lands on a black-eyed Susan and rests there, slowly opening and closing its wings.
The orange and black patterns on its wings are intricate and precise and beautiful.
You watch it lift off and spiral upward until it is just a dark speck against the blue.
The sky receives it without effort, the way the meadow receives the sunlight.

The warmth of the sun rests on your face like a gentle palm.
You feel it on your forehead, your cheeks, your closed eyelids, your lips.
The light behind your eyelids is a warm, golden red, the color of sunlight through skin.
You feel the sun's energy soaking into your body, filling you with slow, radiant warmth.
Each cell seems to absorb the light and glow with it, like the petals of the flowers around you.

A meadow lark sings from a fence post at the edge of the field.
Its song is liquid and complex, a tumbling cascade of notes that spills into the warm air.
The sound rises and falls with a joy that seems to belong to the meadow itself.
Another bird answers from further away, and the two songs weave together across the grass.
The music is as natural as the breeze, as necessary as the sunlight.

The breeze returns, gentle and warm, moving across the meadow in a slow wave.
You see it in the grass before you feel it, a ripple of silver-green passing over the field.
Then it touches your skin, soft and warm, carrying the scent of hay and honeysuckle.
The grass bends and springs back, bends and springs back, like a green sea breathing.
You feel your own breathing synchronize with this rhythm, slow and easy and deep.

A grasshopper leaps from one stem to another, its wings making a brief, dry clatter.
It lands and goes still, perfectly camouflaged against the green, waiting and alert.
The small creatures of the meadow go about their lives around you without concern.
You are part of the landscape now, as still and warm and present as the stones and flowers.
The meadow has accepted you as one of its own.

You feel the vastness of the sky pressing gently down, meeting the earth that presses gently up.
You are held between these two great forces, cradled in the space where sky and ground meet.
This is the most natural place in the world, lying in a field under an open sky.
Every ancestor you ever had knew this feeling, this simple contact with earth and sun and wind.
It is encoded in your body, and your body remembers it now with relief.

A small cloud drifts across the sun, and the light dims to a soft, pearly glow.
The temperature drops just slightly, and you feel the contrast on your warmed skin.
Then the cloud passes, and the full sunlight returns, pouring down with renewed warmth.
You feel the return of the sun like a welcome, like a door opening again.
The meadow brightens around you, and the colors deepen as the light grows strong.

The scent of warm grass intensifies as the afternoon deepens.
It is a complex fragrance, part green and part sweet and part dry.
Mixed with it you catch the honey scent of clover and the faint spice of wild thyme.
These smells enter your body with each breath, anchoring you deeper into this place and moment.
You feel nourished by the air itself, fed by fragrance and warmth and light.

Your body has melted into the ground now, and the boundary between you and the earth feels thin.
Your heartbeat is slow and steady, and the warmth inside you matches the warmth around you.
The sounds of the meadow, bees, birds, wind in the grass, form a gentle, layered lullaby.
You are safe here, completely safe, held by the oldest cradle there is.
The meadow asks nothing and gives everything, and you rest in its open, golden embrace.

When the time feels right, you let yourself return slowly to awareness of your body.
The grass still sways above you, and the sky is still blue, and the sun is still warm.
You carry the meadow's warmth inside you, stored in your skin and bones.
It will be there tonight when you close your eyes, and tomorrow when you open them.
The meadow is always here, always in bloom, always waiting for you to lie down and rest.
