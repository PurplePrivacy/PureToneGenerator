Find a comfortable position and let the body become completely still.
Feel the solid presence of the body, here, alive, real.
Close your eyes and take one long breath in through the nose.
Release it slowly, and feel the body settle deeper into rest.

You are here, breathing, warm, aware.
This moment is not guaranteed; it is a gift you are receiving right now.
Every breath you take is borrowed time, and that is not a grim thought.
It is the most liberating truth available to a human being.

Begin by feeling the breath, just the simple rise and fall of the chest.
Notice how each inhale arrives without being asked for.
And each exhale leaves without being held.
The breath moves through you the way a river moves through a valley, faithfully and without effort.

Now bring your attention to the heartbeat, that steady pulse in the chest.
It has been beating since before you could think, since before you had a name.
One day, this heart will give its final beat, and that moment is unknown to you.
Let that awareness settle gently, without fear, like a stone resting on the bottom of a clear pond.

You are not meditating on death to create anxiety.
You are meditating on death to sharpen the lens through which you see this life.
When you know that time is limited, everything becomes more vivid.
The colors are brighter, the air is sweeter, the faces of the people you love are more precious.

Feel the weight of your body, the warmth of your skin.
These sensations are temporary, and that is what makes them beautiful.
A sunset is not less beautiful because it fades; it is more beautiful for it.
Your life is the same, a brief, brilliant arc of light in an unimaginably vast darkness.

Consider the breath you are taking right now.
There have been millions before it, and there will be a limited number after it.
You do not know the number, and you never will.
Let that unknowing make this one breath feel extraordinarily precious.

Now think of someone you love, their face, their voice, the way they move.
One day, one of you will be here and the other will not.
This is not cruelty; this is the condition of being alive with another being.
Let this truth make the love you feel right now sharp and clear and present.

Feel what happens in the chest when you hold both love and impermanence at the same time.
There may be tenderness, an aching sweetness, a desire to cherish.
This is the gift of death awareness: it teaches the heart to love without postponement.
When you know time is short, you stop saving your love for later.

Think of a meal you enjoyed recently, the taste, the company, the warmth.
There will be a last meal, and you will probably not know it is the last.
Let that make every meal a feast, every glass of water a toast, every bite a celebration.
The ordinary becomes sacred when you know it will end.

Think of a walk you took, the feel of the ground, the light, the movement of your body.
There will be a last walk, a last time your feet touch the earth.
Let that knowledge pour tenderness into every step you take.
The ground has been carrying you your whole life, and one day it will receive you.

Notice the air around you, the temperature, the gentle movement against your skin.
This body that feels the air will one day be still.
But right now, right now, it is feeling everything, alive to every sensation.
What an astonishing thing it is to have a body that can feel.

Now let your attention rest on the simple fact that you exist.
Out of all the vastness of time and space, you are here, conscious, breathing.
The odds against your existence are so large they are meaningless.
And yet here you are, in the center of your own awareness, impossibly present.

Death does not erase the miracle of your existence; it frames it.
A painting needs edges to be a painting; without them it is just color bleeding into nothing.
Your life has edges, a beginning and an end, and that is what makes it a life.
Those edges are not your enemies; they are what give your days their shape and meaning.

Think of the people who came before you, grandparents, ancestors, strangers.
Each of them lived and loved and worried and laughed and eventually let go.
You are not alone in your mortality; every being that has ever lived shares it with you.
To be alive is to be fragile, and to be fragile is to be precious.

Let this meditation soften you rather than frighten you.
When you sit with death gently, as a companion, it transforms into a teacher.
It whispers: do not waste this day, and do not forget to love.

Feel the breath moving through you, each one a tiny affirmation.
Let each breath be an act of gratitude, a quiet celebration of the fact that you exist.
This moment is complete in itself; it lacks nothing.

Now bring your attention to the present moment with all its imperfect beauty.
All of this is happening now, the only time anything ever happens.
The past is gone and the future is uncertain, but this breath is yours.

Think of one thing you have been postponing, an act of love, a conversation, a kindness.
Let the awareness of impermanence give you the courage to stop postponing.
Life does not wait, and neither should your love.
The time for tenderness is always now.

Take a slow breath in and feel the gratitude for being alive today.
Let that gratitude fill the chest, the belly, the whole body.
You are here, and that is enough, that is everything.
When you are ready, open your eyes and look at the world as if for the first time.

Let each thing you see carry the glow of preciousness.
You have been given this breath, this hour, this day.
Go gently, and waste none of it.
