Allow the body to settle into stillness, grounded and at ease.
Feel the weight of the sitting bones pressing into the cushion or chair.
Let the spine rise naturally from that base, neither rigid nor collapsed.
Close your eyes and take three slow breaths, each one a little deeper than the last.

With each exhale, release a small layer of tension you have been carrying.
The shoulders drop. The belly softens. The jaw unclenches.
There is nothing you need to hold up right now.
Let gravity do its work while you rest in the center of this moment.

Bring your attention to the breath at the nostrils, that cool touch of air arriving.
Follow it inward as it fills the lungs, then feel the warm release as it flows out.
This breath is neither pleasant nor unpleasant; it is simply here.
Let it be the anchor for the balance you are about to cultivate.

Now call to mind something mildly pleasant, a warm cup of tea, a kind word from a friend, a patch of sunlight.
Feel how the body responds to this pleasant image, a slight leaning forward, a brightness.
Notice the subtle pull to hold onto this feeling, to make it stay.
See that pull clearly, and then let the image dissolve, returning to the breath.

Now call to mind something mildly unpleasant, a minor frustration, a small disappointment, a discomfort.
Feel how the body responds, perhaps a tightening, a turning away, a contraction.
Notice the pull to push this feeling away, to escape it or fix it.
See that pull clearly, and then let the image dissolve, returning to the breath.

Both reactions are natural, the grasping toward pleasure and the recoiling from pain.
The mind has been doing this since infancy, reaching for what is sweet, pulling away from what stings.
This meditation is not about stopping those reactions but about finding the still point between them.
A place where you can hold both with equal, steady awareness.

Imagine a great mountain, ancient and unmoved, rising from a vast plain.
Its peak touches the clouds and its roots go deep into the earth.
In spring, wildflowers cover its slopes and birds nest in its trees.
In summer, the sun bakes its stones and storms crack the sky above it.

In autumn, the leaves turn gold and fall away, carried off by the wind.
In winter, snow covers the peak and ice locks the streams silent.
Through every season, through every storm and every calm, the mountain remains.
It does not prefer spring to winter or sunshine to rain.

Let yourself become that mountain for a moment.
Feel the base of your body as the base of the mountain, solid and wide.
Feel your spine as the rising slope, stable and rooted.
Feel the crown of your head as the peak, open to the sky.

Seasons of emotion will move through you, as they always do.
Joy will come, and it will pass. Sorrow will come, and it will pass.
Irritation, excitement, boredom, tenderness, all of them are weather.
And you are the mountain, holding still while the weather changes.

Now bring to mind a situation in your life where things are going well.
Feel the natural warmth and gladness that arises.
Hold that feeling gently, without gripping it, as if holding a bird in open palms.
Whisper inwardly: this too is a passing condition, and I can hold it with balance.

Now bring to mind a situation where things are difficult or uncertain.
Feel the unease, the worry, the discomfort that arises.
Hold that feeling with the same open palms, neither squeezing nor dropping.
Whisper inwardly: this too is a passing condition, and I can hold it with balance.

Notice what happens in the body when you hold both experiences with equal care.
There may be a steadiness in the chest, a settling in the belly.
This is equanimity, the ability to be present with what is, without being pushed or pulled.
It is not indifference; it is a deeper form of caring, one that does not break under pressure.

Equanimity sees clearly because it is not distorted by craving or aversion.
It loves fully because it does not depend on conditions for its stability.
A heart with equanimity can hold grief without drowning and joy without grasping.
It stands in the middle of life like the mountain stands in the middle of weather.

Now bring to mind a person you love, and notice the natural warmth that arises.
Hold that warmth with balance, knowing this person has their own path, their own joys and sorrows.
You cannot control their experience, and they cannot control yours.
You can love them completely and still let them walk their own road.

Bring to mind a person you find difficult, and notice what arises.
Perhaps tension, perhaps judgment, perhaps a closing in the chest.
Hold this too with balance, knowing this person is also carried by forces beyond their control.
Their happiness and suffering are shaped by conditions you may never fully understand.

Whisper to yourself: all beings are the owners of their actions.
This is not coldness; it is wisdom, the recognition that you cannot carry another person's path.
You can offer love, but you cannot force outcomes, and that is alright.

Feel the freedom that comes with this recognition.
You do not have to fix everything, and you do not have to be destroyed by what you cannot fix.
You can stand in the middle of a chaotic world with a steady heart.
This steadiness is your gift to yourself and to everyone around you.

Return to the image of the mountain, patient and vast.
The mountain does not cling to sunshine or flee from the storm.

Let this mountain quality settle into your bones, heavy and quiet and sure.
Equanimity is the ground beneath your feet, always present, always enough.

Take a slow breath in, feeling the steadiness in the center of the chest.
Release the breath, and let the mountain dissolve back into your body.
Feel your hands, your face, the ordinary weight of being human.
When you open your eyes, carry this balance forward, one breath, one moment at a time.
