Find a posture that is both comfortable and dignified.
If sitting, let the spine rise naturally, the head balanced gently on the neck.
If lying down, let the arms rest at your sides, palms turned upward.
Close your eyes and take a moment to simply arrive in the body.

You are going to practice the first foundation of mindfulness, awareness of the body.
This is patient, precise, accepting observation, nothing more and nothing less.
You are not trying to change anything you find, only to notice it clearly.
The body is your meditation object, and every sensation is a doorway to presence.

Begin by noticing your posture as a whole.
Feel the shape the body makes in space right now, its angles, its weight, its balance.
Notice where the body touches the surface beneath it, and where it does not.
Simply know: I am sitting, or I am lying down, with full, calm awareness.

Now bring your attention to the breath, just as it is, without adjusting it.
Feel the air entering at the nostrils, the slight coolness as it passes through.
Follow it inward as the chest and belly expand.
Feel the warmth as it flows out again, the gentle collapse of the torso.

When the breath is long, simply know: the breath is long.
When the breath is short, simply know: the breath is short.
You are not making the breath long or short; you are noticing what is already happening.
This is the practice, clear seeing without interference.

If the mind wanders, gently bring it back to the breath.
There is no frustration needed here; wandering is what minds do.
Every return to the body is a small victory of awareness.
You are strengthening attention with each gentle redirection.

Now let your awareness move to the top of the head.
Feel whatever is there, warmth, tingling, pressure, perhaps nothing at all.
Every observation is valid, including the observation of absence.

Move your awareness slowly to the forehead.
Feel the skin there, the muscles beneath it, any tension or smoothness.
The forehead may be tight, or loose, or neutral, and all of that is simply information.

Now the eyes, closed behind the lids, and the cheeks and jaw below them.
Notice if the teeth are clenched or if the jaw hangs slightly open.

Now the mouth, the lips, the tongue resting against the palate.
Feel the moisture inside the mouth, the temperature, the texture.

Move your awareness to the throat and neck, front and back.
Feel the hollow at the base of the throat, the movement of the windpipe with each breath.
Feel the muscles at the back that hold the heavy head upright all day long.
Simply observe, with the same calm attention you would give a stone on a path.

Move to the shoulders, broad and complex.
Notice whether the shoulders are lifted toward the ears or dropped and relaxed.
Just see, just know, without adjusting.

Now the right arm, starting at the shoulder and moving slowly down.
The upper arm, the elbow, the forearm, the wrist, the palm, each finger.
The hand is astonishingly sensitive, and even at rest it carries a map of sensations.
Stay with the right hand for a moment, curious and attentive.

Now the left arm, shoulder to fingertips, the same patient attention.
Notice if the left side feels different from the right.
No need to balance them or make them equal; just notice the difference clearly.

Bring your attention now to the upper back, the broad plane between the shoulder blades.
Feel the muscles layered over the ribs, the gentle expansion with each breath.
The upper back carries posture and often carries emotion as well.

Move downward through the middle back, the lower back, the curve of the lumbar spine.
Observe it with the gentleness you would offer a tired friend.

Now the chest, the front of the torso.
Feel the ribcage lifting and falling with each breath.
The chest is the home of the breath and the heart, two pillars of aliveness.

Move to the belly, soft and receptive.
Feel the belly rise with each inhale and fall with each exhale.
Let your awareness rest here, feeling the gentle movement from inside.

Now the hips and the pelvis, the bowl that holds the core of the body.
Feel the sitting bones pressing into the cushion, or the sacrum flat against the floor.
The pelvis is the foundation, heavy and grounded and stable.

Move your attention to the right leg, from thigh to knee to shin to foot.
The sole of the right foot, the toes, each one individually.
Now the left leg, the same slow journey from thigh to toes.
Feel whatever sensations are present, warmth, coolness, pressure, tingling, numbness.

Now expand your awareness to include the whole body at once.
Feel the body as a single field of sensation, alive and breathing.
Notice the overall quality, heaviness or lightness, warmth or coolness, stillness or buzzing.
The body is not a collection of parts; it is one integrated, breathing whole.

Sit with this whole-body awareness for several breaths.
Everything inside is in constant motion, and yet from the outside, you are perfectly still.

This is the practice, knowing the body as it is, in each moment, just as it is.
Not the body you wish you had, not the body as it was yesterday, but this body, now.
This knowing is itself a form of freedom.
When you observe without judging, the body becomes a place of refuge.

Take one more breath through the whole body, feeling it as a single wave of sensation.
And as you release that breath, let the practice settle into the background.
Open your eyes gently and let the world appear.
Carry this patient, precise attention with you into whatever comes next.
