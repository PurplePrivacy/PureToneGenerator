Find a position where your body can rest with as much ease as possible.
You do not need to be perfectly comfortable to begin.
Whatever you are feeling right now is welcome here.

Start by noticing your breath, just as it is.
Simply observe the inhale arriving and the exhale leaving.
Let the breath be an anchor that holds you steady.

Take a moment to notice what feels good or neutral in your body.
Perhaps the warmth of your hands resting in your lap.
These areas of ease remind you that comfort exists alongside discomfort.

Now, with gentleness, bring your awareness toward the area of sensation.
You are not going there to fight or fix anything.
You are going there the way you would approach a friend in distress.

Notice the sensation without trying to change it.
Is it sharp or dull, constant or pulsing?
Does it have a steady quality or does it shift and change?

See if the sensation has a shape.
It might be round, or jagged, or formless.
There is no right answer; just notice what comes.

Explore whether the sensation has a temperature.
Does it feel warm, hot, cool, or neutral?
Simply observe with the calm attention of a scientist.

See if a color comes to mind when you focus on the sensation.
Let whatever color appears be there without judgment.
This is simply your mind's way of mapping what the body feels.

Notice the texture of the sensation.
Is it tight, heavy, buzzing, throbbing, or burning?
Every quality you notice is useful information, nothing more.

Now begin to breathe gently toward this area.
The breath does not fight the sensation; it simply keeps it company.
Each inhale brings a little space, a little room to breathe.

With each exhale, see if you can soften the muscles around the area.
Not the sensation itself, but the tissue surrounding it.
See what happens when you let those guard muscles relax, even slightly.

Notice if the sensation changes at all when you stop bracing.
Whatever happens is perfectly fine.
You are not trying to make the sensation go away.

Imagine that you could create a little more space around the sensation.
Like widening a circle drawn on the ground.
This spaciousness is a gift you give your body.

Breathe into that expanded space.
The sensation floats in this warm space like an object in calm water.
It is held, not squeezed, not pressured, just held.

Now try speaking to the sensation silently, from a place of kindness.
You might say something like, I am here with you.
Or simply, It is okay.

Pain is your body's way of asking for attention.
It is not punishment; it is communication.
By being present with it, you are answering that call.

Notice what happens in the rest of your body as you hold this gentle attention.
Perhaps your shoulders have softened or your jaw has unclenched.
The body responds to kindness, even in the midst of discomfort.

Now imagine the color of the sensation beginning to shift.
See it slowly changing to something softer, warmer, lighter.
Let this shift happen at whatever pace feels natural.

Feel the warmth of your breath continuing to flow into the area.
Each inhale brings fresh, warm energy.
Each exhale carries away a small measure of tension.
This is not a battle; it is a slow, patient conversation.

Now widen your awareness to include your whole body again.
Feel the areas of ease and the area of sensation together.
You are larger than any single sensation.

You have observed discomfort with curiosity rather than fear.
You have breathed into it with warmth rather than resistance.
This is a skill, and it grows stronger each time you practice it.

Know that you can return to this practice whenever you need it.
You can meet discomfort as a companion rather than an enemy.
Your breath is always available as a bridge between struggle and ease.

Take one more slow, full breath and feel your whole body settle.
When you are ready, open your eyes slowly and take in your surroundings.
Carry this gentleness with you; your body will thank you for it.
