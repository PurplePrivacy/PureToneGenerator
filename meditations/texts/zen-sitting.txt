Take your seat with care, as if this posture were the whole practice.
Let the sitting bones settle firmly, and feel the spine rise from that base.
The back is straight but not rigid, like a tree that bends in the wind but does not break.
Let the chin tuck slightly, the crown of the head reaching gently toward the sky.

Place your hands in your lap, the left hand resting on the right, thumbs lightly touching.
The shape your hands make is a small oval, an empty circle, holding nothing.
Let the shoulders drop away from the ears.
The eyes are half-closed, gaze resting on the floor a few feet ahead, unfocused and soft.

Take one breath and arrive.
You are not going anywhere. You are not preparing for something.
This sitting is not a means to an end; it is the end itself.
There is nothing to attain and nothing to become.

Feel the body breathing.
The belly expands. The belly contracts.
The breath is like the tide coming in and going out on a quiet shore.
You do not push the tide; you do not pull it.

Just this breath.

If a thought arises, let it arise.
You do not have to follow it, and you do not have to push it away.
It is a cloud passing through an open sky.
The sky does not chase the cloud or fear it.

Return to the breath. Return to the posture. Return to just this.

Feel the air entering the nostrils, cool and alive.
Feel the air leaving, warm and released.
Between these two movements, there is sitting, there is being, there is just this.
Nothing more is needed.

The mind will offer you thoughts like a river offers leaves to the current.
Each one feels important, each one wants your attention.
See them, and let them float by.

You are not your thoughts, not the stories, not the commentary.
You are the one sitting here, breathing, awake.

Just sit.

Feel the weight of the body on the cushion or the chair.
Feel the pull of gravity, steady and honest and impartial.
Gravity does not ask what you believe or what you have done.
It simply holds you to the earth, and you can trust it completely.

Notice the sounds around you without naming them.
Sound arises and passes away, like everything else.
Let both sound and silence be just as they are.

When the mind gets busy, do not fight the busyness.
Simply notice: the mind is busy.
That noticing itself is the practice, the moment of waking up inside the noise.

And then return to the body. Return to the breath. Return to just this.

Feel the contact of your clothing against the skin, the temperature of the air around you.
These are not distractions; they are the texture of being alive right now.

The posture holds you. The breath breathes you. The moment offers itself.
Sit the way a stone sits in a field, not waiting, not hoping, just resting.
The stone does not practice being a stone; it simply is one.

Just sit.

Minutes pass, and the mind settles or does not settle.
Both are fine. Both are zazen.
The quality of your sitting is not measured by how quiet your mind becomes.
It is measured by your willingness to be here, breath after breath, without leaving.

Feel the hands in your lap, the thumbs gently touching.
If the thumbs have drifted apart, adjust them softly, noticing and returning.
Again and again, without frustration, without ambition.

There is nowhere else to be. There is nothing else to do.
There is only now, only here, only this breath.

Just this.

The body may ache, the knees may complain, the back may stiffen.
Notice the sensation with the same calm attention you give the breath.
Pain is not failure; it is information, arriving and departing like everything else.

Boredom may arise, the mind declaring that nothing is happening.
Boredom is just the mind wanting more, and zazen is the practice of needing less.
Sit with the boredom until it reveals itself as spaciousness.

Just sit.

This is enough. You are enough. This breath is enough.
The great matter of life and death is not solved by thinking.
It is dissolved by presence, by sitting completely in this moment.
When you are fully here, there is no problem to solve.

Feel the body one more time, solid, warm, breathing, present.
Feel the hands, the posture, the gentle downward gaze.
Nothing has changed, and everything has changed.
You have given yourself the gift of a few minutes of just being alive.

When the sitting period ends, do not rush.
Move the hands, the shoulders, the legs, slowly and with attention.
Walking is zazen. Eating is zazen. Washing dishes is zazen.
There is no separation between the cushion and the world.

Just this.
